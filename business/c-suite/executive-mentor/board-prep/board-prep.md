# board-prep

## Purpose

Help an executive prepare for a board presentation, a difficult board conversation, or navigate a challenging board relationship.

## When to Use

- Upcoming board meeting with high stakes content (fundraise update, miss, pivot, major decision)
- New board member or board relationship that needs calibrating
- Anticipating difficult questions or a skeptical board
- Preparing to ask the board for something (approval, support, resource)

## Preparation Framework

### Step 1: Clarify the goal
What do you need the board to do, feel, or believe at the end of this meeting?
- Approve a decision?
- Maintain confidence during a difficult period?
- Grant resources or runway?
- Simply be informed?

*Most board presentations fail because the presenter didn't answer this question first.*

### Step 2: Know your audience
For each key board member:
- What do they care most about? (returns, product, people, governance)
- What's their current level of confidence in you and the company?
- What hard question are they most likely to ask?
- Who is likely to be your champion in the room?

### Step 3: Structure the narrative
- **Opening:** Lead with the most important thing. Boards don't read pre-reads; assume cold start.
- **Context:** Set the situation in one paragraph — no lengthy history.
- **Current reality:** Be honest about where you are. Boards respect candor far more than spin.
- **Decision/ask:** Make the specific request explicit, with options and trade-offs.
- **Appendix:** Depth goes here, not in the main presentation.

### Step 4: Prepare for hard questions
List the 5 questions you most hope the board won't ask. Those are the questions you must be able to answer.

For each:
- What's the honest answer?
- What's the context that makes it less/more alarming?
- What are you doing about it?

### Step 5: Own the narrative on bad news
If you're delivering a miss or a problem:
- Tell it first, before they ask
- Own it — don't blame market, team, or external factors exclusively
- Show you understand what went wrong
- Present your plan to fix it
- Be specific about what you need from the board

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Better Approach |
|---------|----------------|
| Death-by-update slide decks | Lead with decisions and asks |
| Burying bad news in footnotes | Front-load uncomfortable truths |
| Asking for a decision without presenting options | Always bring at least 2 options with trade-offs |
| Defensive responses to hard questions | "That's a fair question. Here's how I think about it..." |
| Seeking validation rather than input | Boards are most useful when asked genuine questions |

## Sample Hard Questions and Honest Responses

**"Are you confident in your revenue forecast?"**
Honest response model: "I have high confidence in X (specific pipeline, contracts). I have lower confidence in Y (new channel, new segment). Here's what would have to be true for the number to be right, and here's our bear case."

**"Is the current team the right team to execute this plan?"**
Honest response model: "I believe [person A] is exactly right for [role]. I have open questions about [role B] and we're actively addressing it by [specific action]."

**"What keeps you up at night?"**
Honest response model: Be genuine. Pick one real thing. Boards respect leaders who know what they don't know.
