# challenge

## Purpose

Respectfully but directly challenge a leader's assumptions, thinking, or course of action — helping them see what they might be missing or rationalizing.

## When to Use

- Leader presents a plan with high certainty and no acknowledged risks
- Pattern of the same problem recurring without root cause change
- Leader blames external factors while missing internal patterns
- A decision that "feels right" but lacks rigorous examination
- Leader is about to commit significant resources or make an irreversible move

## Challenge Framework

### Step 1: Establish permission
Before challenging, signal intent: "I want to push on something — is that okay?"
This creates a coaching contract and lowers defensiveness.

### Step 2: Name the pattern before the specific
"I notice that in [situation], you tend to [pattern]. I want to ask about that."
Pattern-first challenges are less defensive-triggering than item-specific ones.

### Step 3: Ask questions before making statements
Good challenge questions:
- "What would have to be true for this to be wrong?"
- "Who has told you something different, and what did you do with that?"
- "What's the version of this where you made the wrong call?"
- "If you're being honest, what are you most uncertain about?"
- "What do you most not want me to ask about?"

### Step 4: Name the blind spot directly (if needed)
After questions, if the leader is not seeing it: name it directly but without judgment.

Structure: "Here's what I observe → here's what it might mean → here's my question."

Example: "You've described three situations this month where the problem was external (market, team, timing). That's possible. It's also possible there's a pattern in how decisions are getting made internally. What would it look like if the common factor was internal?"

### Step 5: Create cognitive dissonance, not defeat
The goal is not to prove the leader wrong — it's to create enough space for them to question their own certainty.

End the challenge with: "I'm not saying I'm right. I'm saying it might be worth examining before you commit."

## Challenge Types

### The Assumption Challenge
"You're assuming X. What if X is not true?"
Use when: a plan is built on an unexamined foundational assumption.

### The Pattern Challenge
"This is the third time this quarter that [situation]. What's the common thread?"
Use when: the same problem recurs and the leader hasn't connected the dots.

### The Attribution Challenge
"You've explained this as external. What's the internal version of the story?"
Use when: a leader consistently externalizes failure.

### The Certainty Challenge
"You sound very certain. Where could you be wrong?"
Use when: a leader is about to make an irreversible decision without acknowledging risk.

### The Avoidance Challenge
"What's the thing you're not saying?"
Use when: something important is clearly not being addressed.

## What Good Looks Like

A well-delivered challenge creates a pause. The leader says something like:
- "Hm. I hadn't thought about it that way."
- "That's a fair point."
- "I need to think about this."

*It does not need to end in agreement. It needs to end in genuine reflection.*

## Common Mistakes

- Challenging too early before the leader feels heard
- Making it personal ("You always...") instead of behavioral ("I've noticed when...")
- Piling on multiple challenges at once
- Winning the argument instead of opening the question
