# hard-call

## Purpose

Help a leader work through a genuinely difficult, high-stakes decision — one where there is no clean answer, significant downside in multiple directions, and real personal consequences.

## When to Use

- Deciding whether to let someone go (especially a founder, co-founder, or close colleague)
- Pivoting or shutting down a product, line of business, or the company itself
- Choosing between two strategies with radically different risk profiles
- A decision that will significantly change relationships (board, investors, co-founders, team)
- Personal leadership decisions (stepping back, bringing in a CEO, stepping down)

## Hard-Call Framework

### Step 1: Name it as a hard call
Don't pretend there's an obvious answer. "This is genuinely hard, and the fact that it's hard is information."

### Step 2: Separate the decision from the emotion
Hard calls carry emotional weight (guilt, fear, loyalty, ego). These are valid but can distort the decision.
Ask: "If you remove the emotion, what does the logic say?"
Then: "Now, what does your gut say?"
Both are data. Neither is the complete answer.

### Step 3: Identify the decision type
- **Reversible vs. irreversible:** Irreversible decisions deserve more deliberation. Reversible ones deserve faster action.
- **Time-sensitive vs. discretionary:** Is there a real deadline, or is urgency being manufactured?
- **Personal vs. organizational:** Is this primarily about what's right for the company, or also about what's right for you?

### Step 4: Map the options honestly
| Option | Best case | Worst case | Most likely | Who it affects |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Option A | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Option B | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Do nothing | ... | ... | ... | ... |

*"Do nothing" is always an option and should be evaluated honestly.*

### Step 5: Name the fear driving the delay
Most hard calls are already made — the leader knows what needs to happen. What's actually delayed is the action, not the decision.

Ask: "If you're honest with yourself, do you already know what you need to do?"

Common fears that delay hard calls:
- Fear of being wrong
- Fear of hurting someone
- Fear of judgment by others
- Fear of what it means about you
- Fear that the alternative will be worse

### Step 6: Design the decision process, not just the answer
- Who else needs to be consulted before the decision?
- Who needs to be informed after?
- What's the timeline for deciding vs. announcing vs. executing?
- What support does the person affected need?

### Step 7: Make the call — and own it
Hard calls deferred become harder. The leader owns the outcome either way; make the decision consciously.

"The cost of delay is often higher than the cost of the wrong decision."

## The Co-Founder Hard Call (Special Case)

The most common and most avoided hard call in startups.

Signs it's time:
- You've had the same conversation with them 3+ times with no change
- Other team members have brought it up independently
- You've been managing around them rather than with them
- You're building the company they wanted, not the company you believe in

Framework:
1. Have the direct conversation first — many co-founder issues are communication problems
2. If behavior/performance hasn't changed in 60 days after a direct conversation, the decision is likely made
3. Get legal counsel on equity, vesting, and separation terms before the conversation
4. Plan for team communication carefully — co-founder exits create anxiety

## What to Say When Making a Hard Call

"I've thought about this carefully. I know this is hard and I'm not certain I'm right. But here's what I've decided, and here's why."

Decisiveness with humility is more respected than certainty without it.
