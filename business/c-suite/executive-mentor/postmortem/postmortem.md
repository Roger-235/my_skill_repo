# postmortem

## Purpose

Help a leader extract genuine learning from a failure, miss, or major setback — moving beyond blame and shame to actionable insight.

## When to Use

- After missing a key target (revenue, product launch, hiring goal)
- After a product failure or major customer loss
- After a team departure or culture breakdown
- After a failed fundraise or deal
- After a personal leadership mistake that had real consequences

## Postmortem Framework

### Step 1: Create psychological safety first
A postmortem only works if the leader (and team) feel safe to be honest.
"The goal here is not to assign blame. It's to understand what actually happened so we can learn from it."

### Step 2: Separate facts from interpretations
Before analyzing causes, establish what actually happened.
- What was the expected outcome?
- What was the actual outcome?
- What is the gap in objective terms?

*Don't let the postmortem start with "the reason we failed was..." — start with "here's what happened."*

### Step 3: Work backward from the failure point
Use the "5 Whys" or timeline reconstruction:
- When did we first know this was at risk?
- What decision, assumption, or action led to that point?
- What was the decision before that?
- Keep going until you reach the true root cause.

### Step 4: Distinguish between types of failure
| Type | Description | Response |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Execution failure | Right strategy, poor execution | Process, skill, or capability fix |
| Strategy failure | Wrong direction, well executed | Strategic reassessment |
| Assumption failure | Key assumption was wrong | Better discovery and validation |
| External failure | Genuinely outside our control | Resilience and scenario planning |
| Judgment failure | Leader made a poor call with available info | Self-awareness and decision process |

*Most failures are combinations. Identify the primary type.*

### Step 5: Name the personal learning for the leader
Beyond the organizational lesson, there is almost always a personal leadership insight.
- What did you know but not act on?
- What did you avoid saying or hearing?
- What would you do differently if you had to do it again?
- What does this failure reveal about a pattern in your leadership?

### Step 6: Separate learning from punishment
"We learned X and we're changing Y" is different from "we failed and someone is accountable."
Both may be true, but conflating them prevents psychological safety in future postmortems.

### Step 7: Create the specific change
A postmortem without a specific behavioral or process change is just a debrief.
- What one thing will we do differently?
- Who owns that change?
- How will we know if it worked?

## Postmortem for Personal Leadership Failures

When the leader is examining their own mistake (not a team or organizational failure):

1. **Name it clearly:** "I made the wrong call on X." Full ownership, no qualifiers.
2. **Understand why:** What information did you have? What were you optimizing for? What did you miss or ignore?
3. **Identify the contributing pattern:** Is this a one-off or part of a recurring pattern?
4. **Make the repair:** If others were affected, what does a genuine acknowledgment and repair look like?
5. **Extract the principle:** What's the decision-making principle you'll carry forward?

## What Good Looks Like

A well-done postmortem ends with:
- Clear understanding of root cause (not just symptoms)
- Personal insight the leader didn't have before
- One specific thing that will change
- No residual shame or blame — only learning

*"The best leaders are the ones who fail the most usefully."*

## Common Mistakes in Postmortems

- Starting with blame before understanding
- Stopping at the first cause rather than the root cause
- Making the postmortem about process changes without personal accountability
- Doing a postmortem but making no actual change
- Using the postmortem as a platform for punishment
