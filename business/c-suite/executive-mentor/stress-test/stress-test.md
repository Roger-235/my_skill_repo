# stress-test

## Purpose

Rigorously stress-test a strategy, plan, or decision before major commitment — identifying failure modes, hidden assumptions, and scenarios the leader hasn't considered.

## When to Use

- Before committing to a new strategy or pivot
- Before a fundraise or investor narrative is locked
- Before a major hiring decision (key executive, large team expansion)
- Before a product launch or go-to-market commitment
- Before announcing a major organizational change

## Stress-Test Framework

### Step 1: State the plan clearly
Before stress-testing, get the leader to articulate the plan in one paragraph.
"Explain your strategy/plan as if to a smart friend who knows nothing about it."

If they can't do this cleanly, the plan has a clarity problem before it has an execution problem.

### Step 2: Identify all load-bearing assumptions
List every assumption the plan depends on being true.

Categories:
- **Customer assumptions:** Will customers want this? Will they pay this price? Will they use it this way?
- **Market assumptions:** Is the market large enough? Is timing right? Are competitors doing this?
- **Execution assumptions:** Can our team actually do this? In this timeline? With these resources?
- **Financial assumptions:** Will the unit economics work at scale? Does the CAC/LTV model hold?
- **External assumptions:** What does this plan assume about regulatory, macro, or partnership conditions?

### Step 3: Rank assumptions by risk
| Assumption | Confidence Level | If Wrong, Impact | Testable Before Committing? |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| ... | High/Med/Low | High/Med/Low | Yes/No |

*Focus stress-testing on: Low confidence + High impact assumptions.*

### Step 4: Pre-mortem exercise
"It's 12 months from now. The plan failed. What happened?"

Ask the leader to write or narrate the failure story. This surfaces risks they know but aren't naming.

Common failure stories that emerge:
- "We assumed enterprise customers would close faster than they did"
- "We hired for the company we imagined, not the company we had"
- "The technical complexity was 3× what we estimated"
- "The market leader responded and we didn't have a plan"

### Step 5: Probe the bear case
Most plans are built on a base or bull case. Stress-test the bear.
- "What does the company look like if revenue is 50% of plan in 12 months?"
- "What happens if the key hire doesn't work out?"
- "What if the technical build takes 2× as long?"
- "What if the market shifts in [direction] — does this strategy still hold?"

### Step 6: Identify the kill shot
The one thing, if it's wrong, that kills the entire plan.
"What is the single assumption that, if false, makes this plan unviable?"

This is the assumption to validate first, before committing the rest.

### Step 7: Design a staged commitment
For large bets, recommend staged investment: invest enough to resolve the highest-risk assumption, then re-evaluate.

"What's the minimum we need to invest to learn whether this key assumption is true?"

## Stress-Test Questions by Category

### Strategy
- What would a smart, well-funded competitor do to defeat this plan?
- What are you not doing, and why?
- What does this assume about where the market is going?

### Fundraise
- Can you defend every number in this model under pressure?
- What's the most aggressive due diligence question you'll face?
- What's the story when this quarter's number misses?

### Hiring
- What specifically disqualifies this candidate that you're overlooking?
- Have you reference-checked the reference checks?
- What's your plan if they don't ramp in 6 months?

### Product Launch
- What will your top 3 customers actually do when they see this?
- What does the support/ops load look like at 10× current scale?
- What's the rollback plan if the launch creates problems?

## What Good Looks Like

A well-done stress-test results in:
- A shorter list of load-bearing assumptions, now explicitly tracked
- A modification to the plan that reduces risk on the kill shot
- Staged commitment instead of all-in before the key assumption is validated
- A leader who is more confident, not less — because uncertainty is now named and planned for
